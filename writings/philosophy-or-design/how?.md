pinbreak is a setting that is built upon an earlier setting, called aenyrathia.
this is the second setting in such a fashion, and thus this is the first
description of this. we will first begin with a description of all the preceding
settings, of which of course today there is only one: aenyrathia.

## aenyrathia

aenyrathia was a modern day fantasy setting that i dreamt up and presented to my
players in around January 2024. it was a response towards my previous settings,
which do not bear describing as they 1. had no influence on the design or
evolution of aenyrathia, and 2. were set with medieval to renaissance era
technology, which i felt was different enough in the setting that i could not
draw any experience from them.

the concept for aenyrathia was predicated on the concept that nothing in the
universe is new, it is simply something else with something different. i always
knew i would end up running a modern day technology as my future permanent
campaign setting, but i had to think about what could be the something
different. i realised that all my favourite works in the genre: percy jackson,
harry potter, the dresden files, artemis fowl, all had one quirk - the magic was
hidden. i dug deeper and found that this was true across the genre - all of them
had some sort of secret world, or hidden element to the practicioners or the
wizards or the techologically advanced beings - no one had tried... removing
that.

so i did. aenyrathia is my original brainchild that was predicated on the
question of what would a normal dnd medieval/renaissance era high fantasy
setting look like if it were allowed to grow all the way until it had 21st
century mainstays, like skyscrapers, air travel, globalisation, and cell phones?

however, i can't claim a single ounce of credit for it. i probably found another
setting or a suggestion somewhere that mentioned this quirk and stole the idea
from there. the real people who raised and nurtured this idea were my friends
buzz, haikal, and toph, along with some side characters that ended up not
staying in our lives. we played for about a full year in the world and developed
it to such a degree that we still remember specific character names the party
only met once just because each moment was so developed in our minds.

you can find a more detailed account of the above in the aenyrathia.md article
(#link).

## pinbreak

so whats pinbreak? well, we ended aenyrathia on the 19th of april 2025, for
total runtime of ~13 months. the party played a total of exactly 20 sessions in
that time with an average session length of 5 hours, but usually 4-5, sometimes
up to 8. they went from barely scraping by to probably city level threats. we
started at bare level 1, and ended with them level 7 with 2-3 feats and a magic
item average rarity of rare.

### wait, 2-3 feats? how does that happen

well, that's the reason we decided to start writing pinbreak. by the end, we
were all so jaded with being held back in every possible way by dnd 5e, we
dropped some parts, chopped up others, superscaled one or two, in service of
making our game's mechanics fit the tone and narrative of the scene exactly.

around oct 2025, after we had played i think only two one shots since the end of
the campaign, we played a one shot that was meant to directly tie into the next
"campaign" - i ran this because january (the month we agreed we would start the
next campaign) was fast approaching and i wanted a session -1, per say, an
introduction to the narratives and themes i would like to explore in the next
setting. as usual, we ended up hanging out for a combined 12 hours, with the
last one leaving after i fell asleep, that time divided between planning the
next campaign, discussing what this session -1 should mean, playing the damn
session, and talking about shit we saw lately that was cool (and reminded them
of dnd).

out of the mess of that day was two clear sentiments: 1. i really wanted to
write a new rpg system for the next setting, mostly supported by everyone, and
2. we all loved the idea of letting aenyrathia evolve into a new setting, all
about SUPERHEROES!!!

#### superheros

<insert some images of awesome moments from superheros in film, tv show, web
serial, comic>

talking about this moment a week or so later with toph, we realised why we were
so excited about the concept - the more nerdy hobbies we were into were always
more influenced by millenial culture and interests, purely because thats when
those hobbies became non-cringe. we played dnd because we wanted to play rpgs
and dnd was the most popular rpg, and most importantly, medieval fantasy was the
most popular rpg setting because of it. that means, most people didnt really try
any other settings - the only two other settings i have seen more than a handful
of people my age try is gothic horror (vtm) (modern fantasy LIKE COMIC BOOKS),
and mystery (PULP FICTION??? come on).

if you cant see what im getting at, its that for my generation (1997 to 2006
ish) superheros were our medieval fantasy. comic books were gettin popular thru
the early 2000s, and the mcu started in 2008 - thats PRIME age for something
that we thought was normal in the world and that we probably mildly enjoyed some
aspects of, or knew deeply about some parts of them, or just like them as a
concept and think they were cool, but what is important is the knowledge of the
tropes of the medium (specificially superheros):

- people who don costumes to protect their day to day identity, because they
are, in the end, regular people who want to stay regular people as well as their
caped identity.
- human ingenuity and curiosity eventually leading to supernatural and
fantastical things happening, basically why superhero settings are more grounded
than fantasy settings, like magic, cool tech, future tech, fucking aliens
(literally), and science experiments creating capes, etc
- always a DRIVE to achieve something being central to any superhero story.
stopping a supervillain who is killing civilians, exploring outerspace to see
whats out there, dealing with how humans view those difference from us

elements of the above are also present in modern fantasy novels that were
popular around the 10-18 year range of this age group, like percy jackson and
harry potter, which borrow enough from the genre that they feed into the
understanding of the trope.

what this domain knowledge means, is that you can conceivably take anyone from
this generation and tell them to imagine a superhero story and on average, they
would be better than if you were to tell them to imagine medieval fantasy
stories.

our generation is wired to produce better rpg play when we are playing in
superhero settings because we have this breadth of knowledge (im willing to
bet).

#### pinbreak (for real this time)

so for real, we thought a new system built from the ground up (or i thought)
would be ideal because most of the time, dnd 5e was getting in the way. we
wanted to play quippy characters, but rolling and maneuvering during the combat
minigame of dnd 5e was getting in the way of that, forcing us to delegate
roleplay and combat scenes, sometimes dreading a long slog through combat.

so since clearly dnd 5e wasnt an option, what about other play systems? i
immediately thought that almost every other system i could think of had a
similar albatross in some way, like being so irrecontrovertibility(#sp?) tied to
its setting, or have weird rules that are just meant to so called represent
specific actions probabilities, or have a sort of meta game to it like blades in
the dark where you are a crew on heists. i realised any single rpg system we
picked, we would have to bend to fit our game. the only thing that would change
is the degree of bending it would take and the annoyance caused by that and the
resultant impact on the narrative and the fun.

thus, a new system, unburdened by having to cater to any other groups' play
styles. i started by thinking of powered by the apocalypse having 2d6 against a
varying band by degrees of success and failure, and i liked the idea of
everything being centered around the ACTION. the ACTION is what refers to trying
something that is opposed by something. succeeding usually means making some
progress towards removing that opposition (im being purposely vague here), which
usually means making some progress in the NARRATIVE.

side note: ive always been a fan of rpgs that structure their play sessions as
scenes or episodes from a e.g. tv show. that is how we should play all rpgs!

anyway, thats why i consider each campaign a full narrative, with it having no
defined chapters or arcs (unless the group likes that, mine doesnt) but purely
scenes from start to end. think about something that would be trivial to
transform into a tv show or film or write a campaign recap about, with each
SCENE being under its own header.

so the NARRATIVE is a series of SCENES, which are a series of ACTIONS taken by
characters in the scenes.

##### SCENES

the concept of scenes here is i feel an important one: i plan to clearly define
the start and end of each scene and enshrine it as a mechanic of pinbreak.
before each scene starts, everyone must be in sync, warmed up, and ready to
embody their character. the dm aids in this by:

- briefly recapping the events that led up to the scene
- (optionally if you have a calendar) state the date and time
- describe the location, scents, and sounds of the scene
- have each of the involved parties players' (so the dm does npcs), describe
their party and their objective in the scene
- go time
- after scene, each party explains how their objective was met, or they failed,
or something changed. then, they explain what their thoughts are about what is
coming next.

so this needs to happen to properly make everyone understand the purpose of a
scene: to answer a narrative question. i actually borrowed this concept from
Microscope RPG, which is not really a roleplaying game but more of a narrative
building game, which is why i took it from there. basically, in that game, the
smallest unit of time is a "scene" and that scene must always start with a
question. that scene will take place somewhere in the timeline that you are
building as part of the larger game, and will serve to answer a question someone
else has about the world or timeline, for example "How did King Steen evade his
attempted assassination at the hands of the Lady Box?".

i felt the most important thing we do in playing roleplaying games is to make a
decision that moves the narrative, and protecting those decisions and giving
them structure is the point of the SCENE.

If we do not program in these breaks to change the flow of gameplay, we lose the
player in endless ACTION-taking. remind them it is a narrative focused game and
not a mechanic focused game by making them talk about what they just did and how
it made them feel.

##### ACTIONS

so as we codified above, the core of the game is the ACTION. id first like to
examine why we must have this mechanic, why we cannot just play freeform. the
very way i describe dnd and have for my whole life was to say imagine playing
pretend as a child, but you roll dice when you disagree. there MUST be at least
one mechanic to be a game and qualify for being fun, and this must be random to
force us to improvise with the narrative instead of only relying on each other.

now we have defined the reason we need it, we can understand what the ACTION
should be. as mentioned, i began with 3d6 inspired by pbta's system. i decided
on bands for success and failure. the thing is, we needed these dice rolls to
also represent the struggle for success.
